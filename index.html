mPower Monthly Report

The goal of mPower study is to identify modulators of Parkinson disease (PD) symptoms and to find ways to improve quality of life for people with PD. The monthly report summarizes up to your past 30 days activity scores. It also shows the average scores of participants in your age group who do not have PD (the “control arm” of the study).

The purpose of this report is to let you explore key features of your data and to highlight changes that occur relative to your medication schedule. We encourage you to show your report to your doctor or medical provider to start a dialog about your sensor data.

The monthly report is provided for informational purpose only and is not intended to provide medical advice, diagnose, treat, cure or prevent disease. You shouldn’t use the information that has yet to be validated in place of a consultation with your physician or other healthcare provider.

How many activities did you complete?

The calendar graph below shows whether or not you completed the daily activities pre and post medication. A colored pie piece means you completed the task. Lighter colors represent tasks pre-medication. The darker colors represent task post-medication. The more you do the more colorful your calendar looks. This view may help you see patterns of when you hit your activity goals.

Activity Plots

These plots represent your performance on four different activities: voice, walking, gait/balance, and finger tapping. Out of hundreds of sensor measurements collected through the app, we selected your most relevant ones to plot. The graph shows your most relevant measurement statistics pre and post medication for the past 30 days. Because of the normalization and selection of scores these plots may be different from those shown on the app dashboard.

The horizontal line indicates the average scores of participants in your age group who do not have PD. Activity scores vary from person to person. Some dopaminergic medications can boost the activity score. Good restful sleep and other factors can affect performance and alertness.
